COVID-19

Covid-19 used Molecular Dynamics to simulate how a candidate drug binded to its target.
Scientists ran a simulation of a small-molecule inhibitor docking onto the active site of the SARS-CoV-2 main protease.
The simulation tracked the position of the drug, the orientation, and the interactions that occurred over nanoseconds.
This helped them reveal whether the binding was stable and how strong the drug was held by the hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic contacts,
and whether it may dissociate quickly or not.

Monte Carlo methods were also used in combination with Molecular Dynamics to calculate binding free energy using its algorithm.
This helped rank the drug candidate that had the most chance of success.
By doing this, scientists were able to reduce costs and time in a period where scientists were fighting against time to save people.
This simulation replaced weeks of lab experiments and provided deep insights that experiments could not observe.
The model was CHARMM36-based classical molecular dynamics, with a later ANI/MM hybrid model for more accurate ligand treatment.
